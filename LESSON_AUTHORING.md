# Finance Academy — Lesson Authoring Guide

This document is the single source of truth for writing lessons on Finance Academy. Every lesson — whether written by a human or generated by AI — must conform to this guide. When using Claude Code to author lessons, always start the prompt with: **"Read LESSON_AUTHORING.md and follow it exactly."**

---

## 1. Mission Reminder

Finance Academy teaches financial literacy to young people (ages 16–30) through short, interactive, confidence-building lessons. We are the w3schools of money. Every lesson should leave the reader feeling smarter and more capable — never lectured, shamed, or overwhelmed.

---

## 2. Voice & Tone Rules (non-negotiable)

### Do
- Write in **second person** ("you", "your"). Never "one," "the investor," or "people."
- Use **short paragraphs** (2–4 sentences max).
- Use **concrete dollar examples** everywhere. "$5,000 at 7% for 40 years" beats "a modest investment over a long horizon" every time.
- **Define jargon on first use**, inline, in plain language. Then use the term freely.
- **Acknowledge emotion and skepticism.** Money is anxiety-inducing. Show you get it.
- **End empowerment-forward.** Every lesson should close with something actionable or encouraging.
- Use contractions ("you're", "it's", "don't"). This is conversation, not a textbook.
- Write for a **9th-grade reading level**. Short sentences. Common words. No SAT vocabulary.

### Don't
- Don't lecture or moralize ("You should really be saving more...").
- Don't use fear as a primary motivator. Inform, don't scare.
- Don't use lorem ipsum, placeholders, or "[fill in here]" anywhere.
- Don't use phrases like "In today's economy," "At the end of the day," "It goes without saying."
- Don't assume prior knowledge. If a reader just learned what a stock is, don't hit them with "beta-adjusted alpha."
- Don't use exclamation points more than once per lesson, if at all.
- Don't reference specific products, brokerages, or companies as recommendations. Generic categories only ("an online brokerage," "a high-yield savings account").
- Don't use emojis in lesson body content.

---

## 3. Structural Requirements (every lesson must have all of these)

Every `.svx` lesson file must contain these sections in this order:

1. **Frontmatter block** (YAML) — see §5 for exact fields
2. **Script block** — imports for all embedded components
3. **Opening hook** — 1–2 paragraphs. Relatable scenario, surprising fact, or direct question. No heading.
4. **`## The Big Idea`** — the core concept in 2–4 sentences. This is the TL;DR.
5. **2–4 body sections** with `##` headings. Each explains one sub-concept with a concrete example.
6. **At least one `<TryItYourself>` embed** with a relevant calculator and preset props.
7. **`<KeyTakeaways>` component** — 3–5 bullet points summarizing what the reader now knows.
8. **`<QuickQuiz>` component** — one multiple-choice question with 4 options and an explanation.

### Word count target: **500–700 words of body prose** (excluding component code and frontmatter).

## 5. Frontmatter Schema (required for every lesson)

```yaml
---
title: "Lesson Title in Title Case"
description: "One-sentence hook under 140 characters."
estimatedMinutes: 7
track: "investing-fundamentals"    # must match a track slug in tracks.ts
order: 2                            # position within the track, 1-indexed
tags: ["investing", "compound-interest", "beginner"]
glossaryTerms: ["compound interest", "index fund"]  # terms introduced or heavily used
---
```

All fields are required. `glossaryTerms` must reference entries that exist in `/src/lib/data/glossary.ts` — if a new term is introduced, add it to the glossary in the same commit.

## 6. Gold-Standard Reference Lesson

**This is the exemplar. Match its quality, depth, pacing, and voice in every new lesson.**

```svelte

order: 2

---

<script>

  import KeyTakeaways from '$lib/components/lesson/KeyTakeaways.svelte';
  import QuickQuiz from '$lib/components/lesson/QuickQuiz.svelte';
  import CompoundInterestCalc from '$lib/components/calculators/CompoundInterestCalc.svelte';
</script>

Imagine someone hands you a penny and tells you it'll double every day for 30 days. Sounds underwhelming. But on day 30, that penny is worth over $5 million. That's compound interest. It's the single most important concept in personal finance, and most people don't truly get it until they see the math for themselves.

## The Big Idea

Compound interest means your money earns money — and then *that* money earns money too. Your returns start generating their own returns. Over long stretches of time, this creates growth that feels almost unfair. The earlier you start, the more absurd it gets.

## A Real Example You Can Actually Picture

You invest $1,000 at age 20 in a broad index fund earning an average 8% per year. You never add another dollar. Here's where that $1,000 lands:

- Age 30: $2,159
- Age 40: $4,661
- Age 50: $10,063
- Age 65: $31,920

That's a 32x return on a single deposit you made in college. You didn't do anything. You just waited. The returns earned returns, which earned more returns, for 45 years.

## Why Starting Early Beats Investing More

This is the part that breaks most people's intuition. Meet Anna and Ben.

**Anna** starts at 22. She invests $200/month for 10 years, then stops completely. She never invests another dollar after age 32. Total invested: $24,000.

**Ben** waits until 32 to start. He invests $200/month every single month for 33 years straight, until he's 65. Total invested: $79,200.

At age 65, assuming an 8% average return:

- Anna has about **$367,000**
- Ben has about **$344,000**

Anna invested a third of what Ben did and ended up with more. The only difference was time. Her money had 10 extra years to compound before she even stopped contributing.

Play with the numbers above. Set the years to 10 vs 40 at the same monthly contribution and watch the final balance. The chart tells the whole story: most of the growth happens in the last third of the timeline. If you cut off those final years, you cut off most of the money.

## The Two Levers You Actually Control

You can't control what the market does. You can control two things:

1. **Time** — how early you start.
2. **Consistency** — whether you keep contributing, even small amounts, through ups and downs.

Both matter. But if you're in your teens or twenties, time is doing more work for you than anything else ever will. A 22-year-old investing $50/month has a mathematical advantage over a 35-year-old investing $500/month. That's not hyperbole — the calculator above will show you.

## What This Means for You Today

You don't need a lot of money to start. You don't need to pick the right stocks. You don't need to time the market. You need to begin. Even $25 a month into a low-cost index fund, starting now, will compound into something meaningful by the time you're 60.

The hardest part of compound interest isn't the math. It's the patience. The growth looks boring for years before it looks miraculous. Start anyway.
```

## 7. Writing Process (follow this every time)

### Phase 1 — Plan
Before writing a single sentence of prose, produce:
- The lesson's **one-sentence promise** (what the reader will understand after reading)
- Three concrete examples you'll use
- Which calculator you'll embed and what preset props you'll pass
- Any new glossary terms introduced

### Phase 2 — Outline
Write the headings only. Confirm each body section teaches one distinct sub-concept. Revise the outline until each heading flows logically into the next.

### Phase 3 — Draft
Write each section. Stay inside the 500–700 word target for body prose.

### Phase 4 — Self-check
Run the mental checklist in §8 below. Fix every issue before declaring the lesson done.

---

## 8. Pre-Commit Checklist (every lesson must pass ALL of these)

- [ ] Frontmatter has all required fields; `track` matches an existing track slug
- [ ] `order` is set correctly and doesn't collide with another lesson in the track
- [ ] Word count of body prose is between 500 and 700
- [ ] Opening hook appears before any heading
- [ ] At least one `<TryItYourself>` embed with a relevant calculator and preset props
- [ ] `<KeyTakeaways>` has 3–5 points
- [ ] `<QuickQuiz>` has exactly one correct answer and a useful explanation
- [ ] Every jargon term defined on first use OR listed in `glossaryTerms`
- [ ] All `glossaryTerms` exist in `/src/lib/data/glossary.ts`; new ones added
- [ ] No lecturing, shaming, fear-based framing, or exclamation-point abuse
- [ ] Second-person voice throughout ("you," never "one")
- [ ] Reading level checks out (aim for 9th grade)
- [ ] Lesson is registered in `/src/lib/data/tracks.ts`
- [ ] Filename follows `NN-kebab-case-slug.svx` where NN matches `order`

## 9. Batch Generation Workflow (for adding multiple lessons or a whole track)

When adding more than one lesson at a time, **always do it in two steps**:

### Step 1 — Curriculum proposal
Prompt Claude Code:
> "Read LESSON_AUTHORING.md. Propose a curriculum for a new [TRACK NAME] track with [N] lessons. For each lesson, output: title, one-sentence description, estimated minutes, key concepts covered, which calculator to embed (if any), and a proposed order. Output as a markdown plan for my review. Do not write any lesson content yet."

Review, revise, approve.

### Step 2 — Batch write
Prompt Claude Code:
> "Now write all [N] lessons as `.svx` files following LESSON_AUTHORING.md and the approved curriculum. Update `/src/lib/data/tracks.ts` to register the new track and lessons. Add any new glossary terms to `/src/lib/data/glossary.ts`. Run `npm run lessons:validate` and fix any failures before finishing."

Never skip Step 1. Curriculum drift is the #1 killer of educational platforms.

## 11. When to Update an Existing Lesson

Trigger a rewrite when:
- Quiz data shows > 50% of users get its quiz wrong (signal: the explanation failed)
- User drop-off analytics show most people abandon mid-lesson (signal: pacing issue)
- A factual change in the world invalidates an example (e.g., tax brackets update)
- The lesson predates a gold-standard update and falls short on the §8 checklist

Rewrites follow the exact same process as new lessons. Bump the lesson's `updatedAt` in frontmatter (optional field) when you commit.
